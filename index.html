Drone Forensics & Digital Evidence
Drones operate as cyber-physical systems integrating embedded hardware, wireless communication, onboard sensors, and control software — introducing unique forensic challenges conventional tools cannot address.
Forensic Soundness

UAV as Cyber-Physical Systems

Drone forensics focuses on identification, acquisition, analysis, and presentation of UAV evidence. Unique challenges — proprietary formats, volatile telemetry, removable storage — are not addressed by conventional tools built for computers and mobile devices.

Media Recovery

File Carving Limitations

File system analysis and file carving are less effective on drone storage due to frequent overwriting, fragmentation, and proprietary structures. Literature highlights the need for drone-aware recovery mechanisms on FAT32/exFAT SD and microSD cards.

ML in Forensics

CNN-Based Tampering Detection

CNNs effectively detect manipulation — splicing, copy-move, object insertion. Research stresses ML outputs must serve as decision-support evidence, not definitive proof, especially in legal contexts. This research adopts that principle throughout.

Metadata Analysis

Cross-Correlation for Integrity

Metadata — timestamps, GPS coordinates, device identifiers — can be intentionally manipulated. Validation through cross-correlation with independent sources such as flight logs is essential for evidential reliability in drone investigations.

Chain of Custody

Cryptographic Integrity

NIST SP 800-86 and ISO/IEC 27037 establish that forensic soundness requires non-intrusive acquisition, SHA-256 hashing, repeatability, and documentation — all particularly challenging due to proprietary formats and volatile flight data.

Research Gap

No Integrated UAV Framework

Literature reveals a lack of integrated forensic frameworks combining recovery, metadata analysis, ML-based tampering detection, log correlation, and chain-of-custody management specifically for UAVs. This gap forms the foundation of the proposed research.

GPS Spoofing & Jamming in UAV Systems
GPS spoofing transmits counterfeit signals to mislead drone navigation. Jamming emits radio frequency interference to block legitimate GPS signals — both producing distinct forensic signatures in telemetry logs.
Attack Taxonomy

Spoofing vs. Jamming

GPS spoofing transmits counterfeit GPS signals causing incorrect position, velocity, or time calculation. Jamming emits radio frequency interference causing partial or complete navigation loss. Both produce distinct forensic signatures detectable through telemetry analysis.

Attack Indicators

Common Anomaly Patterns

Common GPS attack indicators include sudden drops in signal strength, abrupt unrealistic coordinate changes, abnormal speed or altitude variations, and timestamp inconsistencies — all detectable via ML anomaly detection applied to telemetry data.

Detection Methods

Current Approaches

Existing approaches include statistical threshold-based deviation detection, signal behavior analysis examining continuity and timing stability, and ML-based anomaly detection. All current solutions target real-time navigation protection rather than post-incident forensic investigation.

Identified Gap

Despite existing detection techniques, most solutions lack forensic-focused, ML-based GPS spoofing and jamming detection systems integrated with visualization and structured reporting. This limits reliable post-incident investigation and evidence presentation, directly justifying the proposed GPS detection module.

Flight Log Recovery & Tampering Detection
Flight logs may be deleted, corrupted, partially overwritten, or stored in proprietary formats — making direct analysis impossible without prior recovery, decoding, and integrity validation.
Telemetry Validation

Decoding Is Not Enough

Decoded flight logs provide structured telemetry — timestamps, GPS coordinates, altitude, speed, heading. However, decoded data alone cannot be assumed authentic. Temporal consistency checks and cross-parameter correlation are required to detect manipulation.

ML Anomaly Detection

Time-Series Pattern Recognition

ML-based anomaly detection effectively identifies abnormal behavioral patterns within time-series data. UAV flight log models can learn normal flight dynamics and flag deviations such as unrealistic acceleration, spatial coordinate jumps, or irregular sampling intervals.

Visualization

Tampering Path Spatial Analysis

Visual mapping of detected tampering paths allows investigators to correlate anomalies with spatial and temporal flight behavior, improving situational understanding and evidentiary presentation. Such visualization significantly enhances expert testimony and post-incident reporting.

Identified Gap

Existing research lacks a unified forensic framework integrating flight log recovery, decoding, ML-based tampering detection, and visualization of tampering paths within a single forensically sound workflow. Most tools address these aspects in isolation, limiting post-incident confidence and evidentiary strength.

Firmware Integrity & Tampering Detection
Firmware governs core drone functionalities — boot initialization, sensor calibration, communication control, geofencing. Malicious modification can enable persistent backdoors and disable safety mechanisms.
Firmware Extraction

Integrity Validation Techniques

Extracted firmware images contain bootloaders, configuration tables, executable code, and embedded metadata. Successful extraction alone does not guarantee authenticity. SHA-256 hashing, version string analysis, entropy profiling, and section layout examination are established validation approaches.

ML Classification

Distinguishing Authentic from Modified

ML models using opcode frequency distributions, entropy patterns, section size ratios, boot sequence markers, and hash deviations can detect firmware modifications. Random Forests, SVMs, Isolation Forests, and Autoencoders have demonstrated success distinguishing benign from modified firmware.

Forensic Correlation

Firmware as Foundation for Trust

Firmware anomalies must be correlated with operational anomalies — abnormal boot behavior, disabled safety features, unexpected communication patterns, or inconsistent flight logs. Firmware analysis provides foundational trust assessments for all higher-level artifacts including GPS data and media.

Identified Gap

Existing research lacks a unified forensic framework integrating firmware extraction, integrity validation, ML-based tampering detection, and forensic interpretation within a single, drone-specific workflow. Most solutions focus on runtime protection and do not meet post-incident forensic requirements for evidence preservation, explainability, and structured reporting.

02. Research Gap

Where Existing Tools Fall Short

Traditional digital forensic tools are built for computers and mobile devices. When applied to UAV incidents, four critical gaps emerge this research directly addresses.

[ GAP — 01 ]

No Integrated Drone Forensic Framework

Literature reveals a complete lack of integrated forensic frameworks combining recovery, metadata analysis, ML-based tampering detection, log correlation, and chain-of-custody management specifically for drones. Tools address these aspects in isolation, limiting post-incident confidence and evidentiary strength.

[ GAP — 02 ]

GPS Attack Detection Lacks Forensic Focus

Despite existing GPS spoofing and jamming detection techniques, most solutions are engineered for real-time navigation protection. There is a significant absence of forensic-focused, ML-based systems integrated with structured visualization and post-incident reporting for legal admissibility.

[ GAP — 03 ]

Flight Log Forensics Remains Fragmented

Existing research lacks a unified workflow integrating flight log recovery, decoding, ML-based tampering detection, and spatial visualization of tampering paths within a single forensically sound system. Most tools address these stages independently, leaving investigators unable to reliably reconstruct drone trajectories.

[ GAP — 04 ]

Firmware Analysis Not Drone-Specific

Existing firmware security solutions focus on runtime protection or generic embedded malware detection, not drone post-incident forensics. Malicious firmware modification can enable persistent backdoors, disable safety mechanisms, manipulate logs, or alter operational limits — making higher-level artifacts unreliable.

03. Research Problem Statement

Current digital forensic tools lack the capability to perform comprehensive, legally admissible investigations on unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs). The absence of a unified, ML-powered forensic toolkit that covers media integrity, GPS attack forensics, flight log tampering detection, and firmware analysis creates critical gaps in drone incident investigations — limiting the ability of law enforcement and security professionals to reconstruct events and present evidence in legal proceedings.

RQ1 · How can ML improve the detection of tampered UAV media files?
RQ2 · What forensic methods effectively identify GPS spoofing attacks post-incident?
RQ3 · How can flight log tampering be detected and visualized forensically?
RQ4 · What approach enables reliable drone firmware integrity verification?
RQ5 · How can all modules be integrated into a legally sound chain-of-custody system?

04. Research Objectives.

What This Research Delivers
